# Warren Buffett Value Investing Methodology

## Philosophy

Warren Buffett's approach to stock picking focuses on finding high-quality businesses trading at reasonable prices with a margin of safety. Unlike technical analysis or short-term momentum, this is fundamentals-based, long-term investing.

> "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." — Warren Buffett

---

## Core Investment Principles

### 1. **Financial Fortress** — Strong balance sheet and earnings power

**Why:** A fortress-like business can survive downturns, reinvest profits, and compound wealth for decades.

**Metrics:**
- **ROE ≥ 15%** — Returns on shareholder capital (not inflated by leverage)
- **ROCE ≥ 15%** — Return on invested capital (more conservative than ROE)
- **Debt/Equity < 0.5** — Conservative leverage; can weather crises
- **Current Ratio ≥ 1.5** — Short-term liquidity safety margin
- **FCF Yield ≥ 5%** — Free cash flow relative to market cap (real cash, not just accounting earnings)
- **Gross Margin ≥ 30%** — Pricing power; not eroded by competition

**How it works:**
- High ROE on low leverage means the company earns well on shareholder money without taking on risk
- Consistent FCF shows the business converts profits into cash (not trapped in inventory or receivables)
- Strong gross margins indicate durable competitive advantage — customers pay premium prices

**Example:** Coca-Cola (pre-2008) had 70%+ gross margins, 30%+ ROE, minimal debt. Even in recessions, people buy soft drinks.

---

### 2. **Durable Moat** — Sustainable competitive advantage

**Why:** Without a moat, any good business eventually becomes mediocre as competition drives down returns.

**Types of moats:**
- **Brand loyalty** — People choose Coca-Cola, Apple, or Hermès by preference (premium pricing)
- **Switching costs** — Expensive or painful to leave (Visa/Mastercard, Oracle software)
- **Network effects** — Value grows with scale (Visa, payment processors, stock exchanges)
- **Cost advantage** — Low-cost producer undercuts all competitors (Costco, GEICO)
- **Regulatory moats** — Protected by law/license (utilities, airports, toll roads)
- **Proprietary data/IP** — Defensible through patents or scale (semiconductor fabs, drug patents)

**Metrics to identify moats:**
- **Margin stability** — Gross margins don't erode even as the company grows (showing pricing power)
- **ROE consistency** — High ROE sustained over 5+ years (not a one-time spike)
- **Unbroken profitability** — Profitable every single year, even in recessions
- **Market share stability** — Customer base doesn't shift to competitors

**How to spot:**
- Ask: "Would customers choose a competitor if the price were the same?" If no → moat.
- Ask: "Can we raise prices 10% without losing half the customers?" If yes → moat.
- Look at 10-year history: Do margins stay stable? Do customers stay loyal?

**Example:** Berkshire Hathaway insurance subsidiary gains $XX billion in float (customer premiums held before payout) — a natural moat because customers stay for decades and the company can invest the float.

---

### 3. **Rational Management** — Incentives aligned with shareholders

**Why:** Even great businesses fail if run by ego-driven or self-serving management.

**Red flags:**
- Excessive executive compensation relative to performance
- Frequent dilutive stock issuance (pumping up share count)
- Bizarre acquisitions at inflated prices
- Poor capital allocation (buying back stock at highs, not lows)
- Lack of owner mentality

**Green flags:**
- CEO owns significant shares (skin in the game)
- Buybacks at low prices, not high (capital discipline)
- Long tenure of management (stability, institutional knowledge)
- Simple, understandable business (no need for aggressive tactics)
- Conservative accounting (not pushing revenue recognition limits)

**Metrics:**
- **Capex/Operating Income ratio** — How much of earnings reinvested? Low = moat. High = capital-intensive/declining.
- **Dividend payout ratio** — Sustainable? Increasing over time?
- **Insider share ownership** — Is management invested in long-term success?

**Example:** Apple under Tim Cook keeps massive cash reserves, buys back stock opportunistically, maintains high margins. Clear owner mentality.

---

### 4. **Valuation Discipline** — Buy at a discount to intrinsic value

**Why:** Even wonderful companies can be poor investments if you overpay.

**Key metrics:**
- **PE Ratio ≤ 20** — Avoid expensive growth stocks. At 20x earnings, you're betting on perfection.
- **Earnings Yield (1/PE)** — What return do your earnings represent? A stock at PE 20 yields 5%; compare to bond rates and growth prospects.
- **Price-to-Earnings-Growth (PEG)** — PE divided by growth rate. PEG < 1.5 = reasonable. PEG > 2 = expensive.
- **Discount to Fair Value** — Stock trading at 25%+ discount to your estimate of intrinsic value = margin of safety.

**Intrinsic value methods:**
1. **Earnings Power Value (EPV)** — Current earnings / risk-free rate. Most conservative.
   - If a company earns $1B per year and treasury bonds yield 5%, EPV ≈ $20B.
   - Stock should trade below this.

2. **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)** — Project 10-year FCF, discount at 8–10% rate, terminal value.
   - More involved but captures growth.

3. **Comparable P/E multiple** — Compare to similar businesses. Margin of safety = buy at 75% of peer multiple.

**Margin of Safety:**
- Never pay full intrinsic value. Always demand a discount.
- Buffett typically wants to buy at 60–75% of estimated value (25–40% margin).
- Protects against errors in estimation and unexpected downturns.

**Example:** If you estimate intrinsic value at $100/share, buy only at $60–75. Leaves room for error.

---

### 5. **Simplicity & Predictability** — Avoid complexity and disruption risk

**Why:** Buffett famously avoids tech and complex derivatives. They require constant re-estimation of moat durability.

**What to avoid:**
- Rapidly changing industries (tech disruption risk)
- Leveraged balance sheets (hidden risk in downturns)
- Complex accounting (hard to audit truthfulness)
- Highly cyclical businesses (requires timing the cycle)
- Cutting-edge technologies with unproven demand

**What to favor:**
- Boring, stable industries (utilities, insurance, basic materials, consumer staples)
- Predictable, recurring revenue (subscriptions, insurance float, brand loyalty)
- Businesses you can understand in one sentence
- Minimal need for capex to stay competitive

**Example:** Gillette sells razors to the same men every month for 50 years. Predictable, boring, profitable. Buffett loves it.

## Buffett vs. Other Screening Approaches

### **Darvas Box (Technical)**
- **Looks at:** Price breakouts from consolidation ranges
- **Time horizon:** Days to weeks
- **Buffett view:** Ignores price action entirely. "Price is what you pay; value is what you get."
- **Use:** Timing entries, but quality must come first.

### **Piotroski F-Score (Quality gating)**
- **Looks at:** 9 financial metrics (profitability, leverage, efficiency)
- **Time horizon:** Last year of financials
- **Buffett view:** Similar to fortress screen, but F-Score is more mechanical (7/9 = pass).
- **Difference:** Buffett digs deeper into moat sustainability and management.

### **Coffee Can (Compounding)**
- **Looks at:** High revenue growth, ROCE, low debt, FCF, market cap floor
- **Time horizon:** 5+ years of history
- **Buffett view:** Aligned! Coffee Can is Buffett-adjacent (high-quality compounders).
- **Difference:** Coffee Can uses thresholds (ROCE > 15%, Rev CAGR > 10%). Buffett is more qualitative about moat durability.

### **Buffett's Own Approach**
- **Looks at:** ROE, moat strength (margin stability, brand power), management quality, valuation
- **Time horizon:** 10+ years of history, seeking 30-year holds
- **Why different:** Emphasizes moat & management above raw metrics. Willing to own illiquid, complex businesses if moat is clear.

## Screening Process (Buffett Screener Implementation)

### **Gate 1: Fortress**
```
✓ ROE ≥ 15%
✓ Debt/Equity ≤ 0.5
✓ Current Ratio ≥ 1.5
✓ Gross Margin ≥ 30%
✓ FCF Yield ≥ 5%
→ Eliminate 90%+ of stocks
```

### **Gate 2: Moat**
```
✓ Margin stability ≥ 85% (gross margin doesn't collapse)
✓ Profitable every year for 5 years
✓ ROE consistency (not a one-time spike)
→ Eliminate another 80%+ of remaining
```

### **Gate 3: Valuation**
```
✓ PE ≤ 20
✓ PEG ≤ 2.0 (if growth > 0)
✓ Discount to fair value ≥ 25% (margin of safety)
→ Only 1–5% of original universe passes all three
```

### **Ranking**
- Quality Score = ROE × Margin Stability
- Discount Score = (Fair Value − Stock Price) / Fair Value
- **Buffett Pick** = Quality Score × Discount Score (quality at a discount)

## Real-World Example: Berkshire Hathaway

**Fortress:**
- ROE: ~20% (via insurance float + stock picks)
- Debt/Equity: ~0.5 (conservative for a holding company)
- Gross Margin: ~30% across insurance + manufacturing
- Float generates $XX billion annually (moat)

**Moat:**
- Insurance: Long-term customer relationships, pricing power
- Utilities (Berkshire subsidiary): Regulated returns, switching costs
- Brand loyalty: "Buffett's reputation" itself is a moat

**Management:**
- Buffett (99 years old, still active): Peak owner mentality
- Buybacks: Only when stock < estimated intrinsic value
- Acquisitions: Only for moat strength (not empire-building)

**Valuation:**
- Buffett buys at 70–80% of book value typically
- Book value/share compounds 19–20% annually
- Long-term price appreciation follows earning power

**Result:** 60-year holding period, 20%+ annual returns, $600B+ value created.

## Where Buffett's Approach Excels

✓ **Stable, mature industries** (consumer staples, insurance, utilities, banks)
✓ **Predictable cash flows** (recurring revenue, brand loyalty)
✓ **Strong competitive advantages** (moats protect pricing power)
✓ **Long holding periods** (compound growth, low taxes)
✓ **Large positions** (can own controlling stakes, influence management)

---

## Where Buffett's Approach Struggles

✗ **High-growth / disruption-prone industries** (tech, biotech) — moat durability unclear
✗ **Cyclical businesses** (commodities, capital equipment) — hard to predict peak earnings
✗ **Rapidly changing competitive dynamics** — yesterday's moat erodes
✗ **Illiquid stocks** — may be unlisted or thinly traded (limits position sizing)
✗ **Short-term volatility** — this approach requires patience; miss the rallies

## Key Takeaways

1. **Buy wonderful companies at fair prices.** Great moat + rational management + reasonable valuation = compounding wealth.
2. **Financial fortress first.** High ROE on low leverage; consistent FCF; strong margins.
3. **Moat durability matters most.** Can competitors steal customers? Can prices be sustained?
4. **Demand a margin of safety.** Never pay full intrinsic value; always demand a discount (25–40%).
5. **Patience is the edge.** Most investors trade; Buffett compounds. Hold for decades.
6. **Simplicity is strength.** Avoid complex businesses you don't understand.
